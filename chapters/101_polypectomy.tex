\section{Colorectal Polyps and Endoscopic Polypectomy}
\label{sec:polypectomy}

\subsection*{Introduction}
In this context, \textbf{polypectomy} refers to the endoscopic removal of
\textbf{colorectal polyps} during colonoscopy. Colorectal polyps are abnormal mucosal growths
that may be benign, precancerous (\textbf{adenomas}), or malignant. Because visual appearance
alone cannot reliably exclude malignancy, resection and histopathological assessment are
often recommended.

Beyond screening, endoscopic polypectomy is a real-time job that asks a lot from the
operator. The clinician has to spot the lesion, judge its shape, choose a resection
method, place the tools carefully, manage bleeding risk, and make sure the removal is
complete. That is exactly why this topic connects so well with intelligent assistance:
the visual input is messy, and the decisions need to stay safe.

\subsection{Clinical Motivation and Procedural Workflow}
Colonoscopy enables direct visualization of the colonic mucosa and supports both
\textbf{detection} and \textbf{removal} of suspicious lesions. In a typical workflow, the endoscopist
first inspects the lesion, then judges the morphology and surface pattern, chooses a
resection method, performs the resection with proper hemostasis, retrieves the specimen,
and finally documents the procedure and schedules follow-up when needed \cite{ferlitsch2017esge,kaltenbach2020usmstf}.

From an engineering perspective, this workflow is sequential: each action (scope
positioning, insufflation, tool deployment, snare closure, cautery settings, clipping)
changes the visual scene and the risk profile. This motivates treating the task as a
\textbf{closed-loop problem} rather than a single-frame recognition problem.

\subsection{Polyp Morphology and Resection Strategy}
Resection strategy depends strongly on lesion morphology. Clinically, polyps are often
described as \textbf{pedunculated} (with a stalk) or \textbf{sessile/flat} (broad-based),
and the estimated size guides whether simple snare resection is sufficient or whether
more advanced mucosal resection is needed.

Important practical factors include \textbf{visibility}, \textbf{access angle}, proximity to folds, and
\textbf{bleeding risk}. These factors are difficult to capture in a single rule, which is why
learning-based systems often model the problem through observed states and iterative
feedback.

\subsection{Common Resection Techniques}
Several techniques are used in colorectal polypectomy. \textbf{Cold snare} is commonly
used for small lesions and avoids thermal injury. \textbf{Hot snare} introduces electrocautery to support hemostasis but requires careful control.
\textbf{Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR)} may be used for larger sessile lesions,
often using a submucosal injection to lift the lesion. \textbf{Endoscopic submucosal
dissection (ESD)} targets en-bloc resection for selected lesions but is technically
more demanding.

\subsection{Recovery and Follow-Up}
Recovery after colonoscopy-based polypectomy is often rapid, but follow-up decisions
depend on pathology and completeness of resection. Resected tissue is typically sent
for histological analysis. Surveillance intervals and further management depend on
\textbf{polyp type}, \textbf{size}, \textbf{number}, and \textbf{margin status}.
This is why post-polypectomy follow-up guidelines matter so much \cite{gupta2020postpolypectomy}.

\subsection{Complications, Safety, and Quality Considerations}
While generally safe, polypectomy can lead to clinically significant complications such
as \textbf{bleeding} and \textbf{perforation}. Risk varies with lesion size and morphology, anatomical
location, technique, and patient factors (e.g., anticoagulation). Incomplete resection
can lead to recurrence and the need for repeat interventions.

From a quality perspective, a key challenge is that many decision points are made under
time pressure and imperfect visibility. This motivates systems that improve visualization
and support consistent decision-making, especially for borderline cases.

\subsection{Implications for Intelligent Assistance}
An intelligent polypectomy assistance system must address both \textbf{perception} and \textbf{control}.
On the perception side, it should help \textbf{localize lesions}, delineate margins, and maintain
stable tracking under motion and occlusion. On the decision side, it must support
sequential actions such as tool positioning, timing, and safe termination conditions.

These requirements motivate the learning methods reviewed in the following sections:
deep models for vision (e.g., \textbf{CNNs}) and sequential decision-making methods (e.g., \textbf{RL} and
\textbf{PPO}) that can be trained and validated in controlled settings before deployment.

\subsection{Summary}
Endoscopic polypectomy is a preventive intervention with a well-defined but
safety-critical workflow. It involves sequential decisions under uncertainty, which makes
it a strong candidate for intelligent assistance methods that combine robust perception
with stable decision-making.
