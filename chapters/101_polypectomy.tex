\section{Colorectal Polyps and Endoscopic Polypectomy}
\label{sec:polypectomy}
\subsection{What is a Polyp?}
A \textbf{colorectal polyp} is an abnormal growth from the lining of the colon or rectum. It can be small and flat or larger and on a stalk. The most common precancerous type is called an \textbf{adenoma}, which is a glandular polyp with a higher risk of becoming cancer over time, so it is often removed during colonoscopy even when it does not yet cause symptoms.

This is why polyp size, shape, and appearance matter in practice: the endoscopist has to decide quickly whether the lesion should be removed and how much risk is involved.

After removal, polyps are sent for \textbf{histology} to check for cancer or high‑risk features. \textbf{Small polyps} are often removed during the same colonoscopy, while larger or complex lesions may need \textbf{advanced techniques} or referral. The choice balances \textbf{completeness} of removal and patient \textbf{safety}.

Modern imaging modes such as \textbf{NBI} or simple dye techniques can show small surface patterns. These patterns help the clinician decide if a lesion looks suspicious and which removal method is best.

\subsection{What is an Endoscope?}
An \textbf{endoscope} is a flexible tube with a camera and light source at the tip, used to visualize the interior of the gastrointestinal tract. During colonoscopy, the endoscope is inserted through the rectum and advanced through the colon, allowing the clinician to inspect the mucosa for abnormalities such as polyps. The endoscope also has channels that allow the passage of tools for biopsy or polypectomy. The endoscopist controls the movement of the scope, the camera angle, and the tools, making it a complex task that requires real-time decision-making based on visual feedback.

A modern endoscope gives a \textbf{live video feed} and allows the operator to control \textbf{tip deflection} and the viewing angle. Important functions include \textbf{insufflation} to open the lumen, \textbf{suction} to clear fluids, and the \textbf{instrument channel} to pass snares or forceps. Good \textbf{image quality} and steady positioning make detection and safe treatment easier.

\subsection{What is Colonoscopy?}

A \textbf{colonoscopy} is a procedure that uses a flexible tube with a camera to examine the colon. It is used for \textbf{screening}, \textbf{diagnosis} and \textbf{treatment} (for example, polyp removal). Good \textbf{bowel preparation} and careful\textbf{ navigation} are needed so the doctor can see the mucosa clearly.

Main steps: \textbf{prepare}, \textbf{insert and reach the cecum}, \textbf{inspect while withdrawing}, and \textbf{treat or sample} lesions. Quality measures include \textbf{cecal intubation rate} and \textbf{adenoma detection rate}. Major risks are post-procedure bleeding and rare perforation.

A typical colonoscopy uses \textbf{light sedation} and lasts about \textbf{20--40 minutes}. If a polyp is removed, the operator may use \textbf{clips or cautery} to control bleeding. Final care and follow-up depend on \textbf{histology and lesion size}; clear written instructions are given to the patient before discharge.

\subsection{What is Polypectomy?}
A \textbf{polypectomy} is the endoscopic \textbf{resection} (removal) of \textbf{colorectal polyps} during colonoscopy. Polyps may be benign, precancerous (\textbf{adenomas}), or malignant. Because appearance alone cannot exclude malignancy, \textbf{resection} and \textbf{histopathological assessment} are often recommended.

A \textbf{polypectomy} is therefore a subset of \textbf{endoscopy} — procedures that use an \textbf{endoscope} to visualise and sometimes treat the gastrointestinal tract.

Beyond screening, polypectomy is a \textbf{real-time} procedure that requires the operator to: \textbf{spot the lesion}, \textbf{judge its shape}, select the \textbf{resection method}, \textbf{position tools}, \textbf{manage bleeding risk}, and ensure the \textbf{removal is complete}. This is why reliable \textbf{perception} and safe \textbf{decision-making} are essential.

\subsection{Resection Techniques}
Resection strategy depends on lesion morphology (for example, \textbf{pedunculated} vs \textbf{sessile/flat}) and on practical factors such as \textbf{visibility}, \textbf{access angle}, proximity to folds, and \textbf{bleeding risk}. These aspects guide the choice between simpler and more advanced cutting methods \cite{ferlitsch2017esge,ESGE2024_Polypectomy_Update}.

Common techniques and when to use them:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cold snare}: best for small lesions; it removes tissue without electrocautery and thus reduces thermal injury.
\item \textbf{Hot snare}: uses electrocautery to cut and help haemostasis; useful for selected lesions but carries thermal risk.
\item \textbf{Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR)}: for larger sessile lesions; often pairs a submucosal injection with snare resection and can be done piecemeal.
\item \textbf{Endoscopic submucosal dissection (ESD)}: enables en-bloc resection for selected complex lesions; offers better margins but is technically demanding and slower.
\end{itemize}

Recent randomized trials and pooled analyses have directly compared cold and hot snare for small polyps; these studies show different trade-offs in delayed bleeding and completeness of resection \cite{Chang2023_AnnInternMed,Liu2023_IntJColorectalDis,Abuelazm2023_JClinGastro,Niu2023_JGastroHepatol}.

Choosing a technique balances \textbf{completeness} (low recurrence) against \textbf{procedural risk} (bleeding, perforation) and required operator skill. For guidance on EMR and ESD indications and technique, see ESGE guidance and ESD reviews \cite{ferlitsch2017esge,PimentelNunes2015_ESGE_ESD,ESGE2024_Polypectomy_Update}. Learning-based systems should model these trade-offs so policies can select safe, effective actions under uncertainty.

\subsection{Cold vs Hot snare}
\label{sec:cold_hot_snare}
Cold and hot snare are similar in use but different in how they cut.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cold snare}: no electrocautery, lower risk of thermal injury, best for \textbf{small} polyps.
\item \textbf{Hot snare}: uses electrocautery, can help stop bleeding but adds \textbf{thermal risk}; used for selected lesions.
\end{itemize}
