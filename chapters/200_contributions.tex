\section{Introduction}
In this chapter we'll document our main technical contribution: a \textbf{two-phase} Unity simulation that uses deep reinforcement learning for \textbf{endoscope navigation} and \textbf{snare capture}.
Robotic or assisted endoscopy requires two main capabilities: \textbf{robust navigation} through constrained anatomical spaces, and \textbf{precise manipulation} for therapeutic actions (like polyp resection). The colon, with its 2,000 cm\textsuperscript{2} internal surface area, is exceptionally difficult to navigate. The gastrointestinal tract presents profound \textbf{perceptual challenges}. The mucosal surface is highly reflective, deformable, and often obscured by fluids or peristaltic movement, making \textbf{visual tracking} notoriously difficult. Furthermore, \textbf{safe maneuvering} requires a delicate balance: applying enough force to advance without causing tissue damage or patient discomfort. Training an autonomous system to handle these unpredictable nuances in the real world would be dangerous and ethically unacceptable. Therefore, our \textbf{simulated environment} serves as an indispensable first step, providing a safe, repeatable sandbox for iterative algorithm development. To make the learning process tractable for our AI, we split the work into two distinct phases: \textbf{Phase~1 (navigation)} and \textbf{Phase~2 (snare capture)}. Separating these tasks simplifies training, dramatically reduces the exploration burden, and enables us to evaluate each capability clearly.

\textbf{What this project is, and what it is not:}
Let's be clear upfront. This project features \textbf{vision-only} agents that learn purely from camera images. We are not using any explicit polyp detector or segmentation model. Moreover, this is a simulated environment with simplified physics and geometric polyp models, meaning it is perfect for algorithmic development, but it's not a clinical substitute ready to be tested on humans tomorrow!

In this chapter, we'll dive into how we built this virtual world, the action interfaces for our agents, how we shaped their rewards to prevent them from crashing, and the neural network brains powering the whole operation.
