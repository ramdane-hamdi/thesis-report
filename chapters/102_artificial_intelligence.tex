\section{Artificial Intelligence for Endoscopic Decision Support}
\label{sec:artificial_intelligence}

Artificial Intelligence (\textbf{AI}) refers to computational methods that can perform tasks
usually associated with human intelligence, such as \textbf{perception}, \textbf{reasoning}, \textbf{learning}, and
\textbf{decision-making}. In practice, modern AI systems in healthcare are mostly data-driven:
they learn patterns from curated datasets and are judged with \textbf{task-specific metrics}
\cite{goodfellow2016deep,litjens2017dlSurvey}. That's why this work uses
\textbf{reinforcement learning (RL)} together with computer vision and temporal models: specifically,
policy optimization methods (e.g. Proximal Policy Optimization, PPO), convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
for visual perception, and recurrent models such as LSTM for temporal context. This combination is a natural
fit for the sequential decision-making problem of polypectomy. We will cover the relevant AI approaches
in more detail in the next sections, but here we give a high-level overview of how AI is used in endoscopy
and what the main challenges are.


For endoscopy and polypectomy, AI is primarily used to (i) interpret endoscopic video
streams, and (ii) support consistent decisions under time pressure. This includes
detecting lesions, delineating margins, estimating uncertainty, and supporting
procedure-level decisions that depend on sequential context. Randomized controlled
trials have shown that real-time computer-aided detection can increase polyp and
adenoma detection rates during colonoscopy \cite{wang2019cade,repici2020cade}.

Simulation platforms and general RL/agent toolkits (like \textbf{Unity ML-Agents}) are often
used to prototype interactive assistance systems and train policies in controlled
environments \cite{juliani2018unity}.

\subsection{What parts of AI concern us?}
The AI landscape is huge, but the following approaches are the ones that matter most
for intelligent endoscopic assistance:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Deep Learning (DL):} neural networks such as \textbf{CNNs} for image-level
  perception and \textbf{LSTM} for modelling temporal dependencies in video.
  \item \textbf{Computer Vision (CV):} extracting information from images (detection,
  segmentation, tracking) using CNNs; outputs can feed downstream decision modules.
  \item \textbf{Reinforcement Learning (RL):} learning sequential decision policies from
  interaction and feedback; here we use policy optimisation (PPO) to train real-time,
  robust action policies for navigation and snare control.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{AI tasks for Polypectomy}
Our goal is to develop an agent (or several cooperating agents) that can perform two core tasks required for safe, effective polypectomy:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Navigate:} guide and position the endoscope to provide a stable, well-framed view of the lesion. This includes steering the tip around folds and flexures, reducing looping, managing insufflation, maintaining an occlusion-free line of sight, and keeping the target within reach for tool deployment.
  \item \textbf{Extract:} execute tissue resection safely and effectively. This covers precise snare placement and closure, choosing the appropriate resection method (cold snare, hot snare, EMR, or ESD), controlling energy delivery when used, managing immediate complications such as bleeding or perforation, and ensuring specimen retrieval for histology.
\end{itemize}

These capabilities map directly to the perception + decision + control pipeline described above: perception (CNN) supplies lesion location and margins, temporal models (LSTM) provide context and stability across frames, and the RL policy (PPO) issues navigation and tool-control actions under safety constraints.

\subsection{Constraints: Safety, Robustness, and Evaluation}
Clinical deployment imposes constraints that strongly influence model design and
evaluation:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Safety:} false negatives can be clinically costly, while false positives can
  distract and reduce trust.
  \item \textbf{Domain shift:} models must generalize across devices, centers, patient
  populations, and imaging conditions.
  \item \textbf{Latency:} assistance must be real-time and stable (avoid flickering outputs).
  \item \textbf{Interpretability and usability:} outputs must be understandable and useful
  during the procedure.
\end{itemize}

These constraints push us toward system-level evaluation, not just per-frame accuracy,
and they also make the link between perception and decision-making much tighter.

\subsection{Summary}
AI is the big umbrella under which both vision-based perception and sequential
decision-making sit. The next section focuses on reinforcement learning, which is a
very natural way to model procedural decisions.
